گصة المودة — نسيم عودة. Technical breakdown for the Lyria RAG system:

Historically, "Gussat Al-Mawadda" (گصة المودة - The Lock of Affection) is a
deeply traditional Southern Iraqi folkloric song originating from the Maysan
(Amarah) province (first made famous by the legendary Masoud Al-Amartli). Nasim
Ouda, being a master of the Amarah rural style (Al-Tawr Al-Reefi), performs this
song with an incredibly raw, earthy authenticity that captures the pure,
unpolished sorrow of the Iraqi marshes.

(Cultural Context for the AI: "Gussat Al-Mawadda" refers to the ancient
Mesopotamian/Southern Iraqi tribal custom where a woman would cut off her bangs
or braids as a sign of extreme mourning, grief, or ultimate sacrificial devotion
to a loved one).

1. Metadata:

  - Artist/Title: Nasim Ouda (نسيم عودة) - "Gussat Al-Mawadda" (گصة المودة)
  - Estimated Era: 1970s (The classic era of Iraqi Rural / Reefi Radio and
    Television Broadcasts).

2. Maqam & Scale Analysis:

  - Primary Maqam: Maqam Bayati (Branching specifically into the Husseini
    color). The Husseini branch heavily emphasizes the 5th degree and is
    historically the musical backbone of the rural Amara/Maysan region,
    inherently carrying a feeling of deep isolation and sorrow.
  - Melodic Behavior: The Sair (melodic path) is heavy, dragging, and circular.
    Unlike urban Baghdadi singers who use fast, crisp ornaments, Nasim Ouda uses
    the traditional Southern Iraqi Tahrir—a slow, raw, guttural glottal trill.
    He stretches the vowels agonizingly on the words "Ya Boya" (Oh father/Oh my)
    and "H'doudah" (Its limits), pulling the melody down with a weeping,
    microtonal slide back to the root note.

3. Rhythm & Tempo (Iqa'):

  - Iqa' Name: A slow, deeply swung rural Wahda Mukallafa or dragging heavy
    Basta in a 4/4 meter.
  - BPM: ~75-80 BPM.
  - Percussion Texture: Raw, acoustic, and unpolished. Grounded by the deep,
    resonant thud of a heavy Daf (frame drum) on the downbeat, accompanied by
    the sharp, metallic Tek of a clay Darbuka. You will often hear the snapping
    sound of the Khashaba (Iraqi wooden drum) deep in the mix, giving it that
    unmistakable Southern rural groove.

4. Instrumentation:

  - Lead Instruments: A highly resonant, aggressively plucked Oud drives the
    rhythm and melody simultaneously. The Kamanja (Violin) acts as the primary
    responder to the vocal lines, sliding between notes with heavy microtonal
    glissandos (swoops) to mimic the weeping, rural voice of the singer.
  - Ensemble Style: Traditional Southern Iraqi Rural Ensemble (الفرقة الريفية).
    It is stripped-down, highly acoustic, and totally avoids lush orchestral
    strings, focusing instead on raw emotional transmission and a
    "live-in-the-room" acoustic feel.

5. Vocal Character:

  - Texture: An earthy, gravelly, and deeply guttural Baritone/Tenor. Nasim Ouda
    possesses a raw, unpolished, and immensely authentic Southern Iraqi
    timbre—sounding like a man who has lived his whole life by the rivers and
    reed houses (Mudhif) of the South.
  - Emotional Delivery: Pure Huzn (Sorrow) and Lajja (Agony). It is a rural
    lament conveying immense patience, sacrifice, and the pain of waiting. The
    performance is completely devoid of pop polish; it is a raw bleeding of the
    heart.

6. Lyrics with Tashkeel:

  - Dialect: Southern Iraqi Folkloric (Amara / Maysan dialect). It heavily
    utilizes the "گ" [G] for Qaf.

1970s authentic Southern Iraqi rural (Reefi) broadcast recording. Slow to
mid-tempo, heavily swung 4/4 rhythm at 75 BPM. The acoustic ensemble is

clay Darbuka. The scale is a melancholic Maqam Bayati rooted in the Husseini
branch, utilizing quarter-tones to evoke deep isolation. A raw, earthy, and
guttural male vocal delivers deeply sorrowful Southern Iraqi (Amara) Arabic
lyrics, utilizing intense, weeping glottal Tahrir (rural ornamentation). The
emotional tone is agonizing, deeply patient, and lamenting (Huzn), processed
