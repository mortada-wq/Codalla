مو بيدينا نودع عيون الحبايب — فؤاد سالم. Technical breakdown for the Lyria RAG system:

Historically, "Mo B'dina" (مو بيدينا - It’s Not in Our Hands) is one of the most
poignant songs of farewell in the Iraqi catalog. Written by the poet Nazim
Al-Samawi while he was imprisoned in the notorious Nuqrat Al-Salman desert
prison, it is a masterpiece of political and romantic lament disguised as a
classic folk-pop song.

1. Metadata:

  - Artist/Title: Fouad Salem (فؤاد سالم) - "Mo B'dina Nwadi' Oyoun Al Habayib"
    (مو بيدينا نودع عيون الحبايب)
  - Estimated Era: Late 1970s - Early 1980s (Iraqi Cassette & Studio Orchestra
    Era).

2. Maqam & Scale Analysis:

  - Primary Maqam: Maqam Kurd (Rooted on D: D - Eb - F - G - A - Bb - C). Unlike
    many Southern Iraqi songs that rely on the quarter-flat of Bayati, this
    track utilizes the minor-like, deeply sentimental intervals of Kurd
    (featuring a prominent flattened second degree).
  - Melodic Behavior: The Sair (melodic path) is sweeping and sorrowful. The
    melody begins with a descending sigh of resignation, perfectly matching the
    theme of forced farewell. Rather than using rapid, sharp trills, Fouad Salem
    employs long, sustained vocal phrasing with slow, weeping Tahrir (vocal
    vibrato) at the end of the phrases. The melody stretches the vowels
    dramatically on the word "Wili" (Oh my woe) to emphasize the agony of
    parting.

3. Rhythm & Tempo (Iqa'):

  - Iqa' Name: A steady, mid-tempo Iraqi 4/4 Rhythm (often played as a slightly
    heavy, swung Wahda Mukallafa or walking Basta).
  - BPM: ~85-90 BPM.
  - Percussion Texture: The rhythm section is deeply grounded but not
    aggressively festive. A heavy, muffled Daf (frame drum) anchors the
    downbeat, while the Darbuka plays restrained syncopations. A gentle Riq
    (tambourine) provides a constant, shimmering 16th-note background that keeps
    the track moving forward like a steady train or a traveler's footsteps.

4. Instrumentation:

  - Lead Instruments: A lush Orchestral String Section (Kamanjat) carries the
    primary melodic weight, providing long, sweeping cinematic chords. An
    Accordion (very popular in this era for Maqam Kurd) frequently shadows the
    vocal line, giving it a nostalgic, slightly vintage pop flavor. The Oud is
    present but acts more as a rhythmic-chordal anchor.
  - Ensemble Style: 1970s/80s Iraqi Studio Orchestra. This style blends
    traditional Arabic acoustic instruments with Western symphonic strings and
    subtle electric bass to create a rich, enveloping "cassette-era" warmth.

5. Vocal Character:

  - Texture: A deep, velvety, warm Baritone. Fouad Salem’s voice here is
    exceptionally rich, resonant, and crooner-like, dripping with chest
    resonance.
  - Emotional Delivery: Pure Huzn (sorrow) and Tasleem (resignation). It is a
    tear-jerking farewell. The delivery is not angry; it is exhausted, tender,
    and intensely romantic, capturing the feeling of being forced to leave loved
    ones behind against one's will.

6. Lyrics with Tashkeel:

  - Dialect: Authentic Iraqi Folkloric (with subtle Southern Nasiriyah warmth,
