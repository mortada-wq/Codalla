هلوا واحنه نهل — فاضل عواد. Technical breakdown for the Lyria RAG system:

(Note: this is the legendary Dr. Fadhel Awad (فاضل عواد), the original artist of
this iconic Iraqi masterpiece, with lyrics by the poet Taher Salman.)

Historically, "Hallou W'ahna Nhal" (1978) is a masterclass in joyful Iraqi
folkloric music. It features one of the most famous Oud intros in Iraqi history
(played by the great Ali Al-Imam) and is the definitive anthem of Iraqi reunions
and celebrations.

1. Metadata:

  - Artist/Title: Fadhel Awad (فاضل عواد) - "Hallou W'ahna Nhal" (هلوا واحنه
    نهل)
  - Estimated Era: Late 1970s (Released around 1978 during the Golden Age of the
    Iraqi Radio and Television Orchestra).

2. Maqam & Scale Analysis:

  - Primary Maqam: Rooted heavily in a festive Maqam Bayati (D - E-half-flat - F
    - G - A - Bb - C).
  - Melodic Behavior: The Sair (melodic path) is circular, energetic, and highly
    inviting. Instead of the typical melancholic descent associated with Iraqi
    Bayati, this track emphasizes the brighter intervals to encourage group
    participation. It opens with an iconic, rapid, staccato Oud solo before
    dropping into a highly rhythmic, call-and-response vocal melody. Fadhel Awad
    uses precise, tight Tahrir (ornamentation)—avoiding long, sad melismas in
    favor of rhythmic, bouncing vocal trills that keep the momentum moving
    forward.

3. Rhythm & Tempo (Iqa'):

  - Iqa' Name: Fast Iraqi Basta blended with an upbeat Chobi (a very
    rhythmic 4/4 meter heavily accented on the off-beats for dancing).
  - BPM: ~105-115 BPM.
  - Percussion Texture: A driving, incredibly festive rhythm section. It is
    completely dominated by the sharp, snapping crack of the Khashaba (Iraqi
    wooden drum/Kasar), a heavy metallic Tek from the clay Darbuka, a steady
    Daf, and most importantly: prominent, rhythmic Safga (communal handclaps)
    which give it a massive wedding-like energy.

4. Instrumentation:

  - Lead Instruments: The Oud is the absolute star of the arrangement,
    delivering rapid rhythmic plucking rather than slow improvisation. The Qanun
    provides bright, festive cascades behind the vocals, and orchestral Violins
    (Kamanjat) play rich, unison melody lines with the chorus.
  - Ensemble Style: 1970s Iraqi Radio & TV Orchestra (الفرقة الموسيقية للإذاعة
    والتلفزيون). It is a large, acoustic folkloric ensemble designed to sound
    like a massive communal gathering, complete with a robust backing choir.

5. Vocal Character:

  - Texture: A brilliant, polished, academic Baritone/Tenor. Dr. Fadhel Awad is
    historically praised for his "crystal-clear diction" (نقاء اللفظ) and a
    cultured, highly trained resonance that never loses its traditional Iraqi
    flavor.
  - Emotional Delivery: Pure Farah (Joy) and communal celebration. Unlike the
    deep Huzn (sorrow) of Southern laments, this is a song of welcoming, reunion
    (لم الشمل), and ecstatic happiness. It is bright, energetic, and deeply
    uplifting.

Late 1970s Iraqi golden age radio broadcast, fast 4/4 Iraqi Chobi and Basta
rhythm at 110 BPM. The arrangement features a highly festive traditional Iraqi
orchestra driven by a rapid, staccato acoustic Oud, bright Qanun glissandos, and
a massive percussion section centered around a snapping Khashaba wooden drum,
Darbuka, Daf, and communal rhythmic handclaps. The melody is rooted in a joyful,
danceable Maqam Bayati with a circular, call-and-response structure. A polished,
resonant male baritone vocal delivers clear Baghdadi Arabic lyrics celebrating
reunion, supported by a large mixed backing choir. The emotional tone is
energetic, communal, and uplifting—resembling a massive traditional Iraqi
wedding celebration—processed with authentic wide acoustic room reverberation
