حبينا ضي الگمر — فؤاد سالم. Technical breakdown for the Lyria RAG system:

Historically, "Habeina Dhay Al-Kamar" (حبينا ضي الگمر - We Loved the Light of
the Moon) is one of the most iconic romantic ballads in the Iraqi repertoire.
Composed by Dhiab Khalil with lyrics by the poet Dawood Al-Ghannam, it perfectly
captures the 1980s Iraqi transition from purely rural folk into sophisticated,
deeply romantic orchestral pop.

1. Metadata:

  - Artist/Title: Fouad Salem (فؤاد سالم) - "Habeina Dhay Al-Kamar" (حبينا ضي
    الگمر)
  - Estimated Era: Early 1980s (The pinnacle of the Iraqi Studio Orchestra and
    Cassette Era).

2. Maqam & Scale Analysis:

  - Primary Maqam: Maqam Kurd (Rooted on D: D - Eb - F - G - A - Bb - C). It
    relies on the minor-leaning, deeply sentimental flattened second degree
    rather than the quarter-tones of Bayati, giving it a universal, nocturnal
    sweetness.
  - Melodic Behavior: The Sair (melodic path) is highly nostalgic and gently
    descending. It mimics the gentle swaying of a boat (as heavily referenced in
    the lyrics about "shores and sails"). Fouad Salem utilizes slow, drawn-out
    phrasing, stretching the vowels in a romantic, pleading manner. He employs
    his signature chest-resonant vibrato heavily on the word "Nasina" (forgot
    us) to emphasize the gentle heartbreak of unrequited loyalty.

3. Rhythm & Tempo (Iqa'):

  - Iqa' Name: A slow, swaying Iraqi Wahda or highly relaxed Basta (a steady 4/4
    meter).
  - BPM: ~75-80 BPM.
  - Percussion Texture: Highly atmospheric and restrained. The rhythm section
    creates a gentle "rocking" or "swaying" motion. A soft, heartbeat-like Daf
    handles the downbeat (Dum), while a muted clay Darbuka and a delicate Riq
    provide gentle 16th-note syncopations that never overpower the sweeping
    vocals.

4. Instrumentation:

  - Lead Instruments: Lush, sweeping Orchestral Violins (Kamanjat) provide a
    cinematic, romantic backdrop. The Qanun is highly prominent here, delivering
    sparkling, starlit glissandos that musically mimic the "light of the moon"
    theme. The Oud plays a steady rhythmic-melodic anchor role.
  - Ensemble Style: 1980s Iraqi Studio Orchestra. It blends a massive,
    classically trained Arab string section with subtle, warm 1980s analog
    studio bass to thicken the low-end frequencies.

5. Vocal Character:

  - Texture: A deep, velvety, warm Baritone. Fouad Salem acts almost as an Iraqi
    "crooner" here; his voice is incredibly resonant, carrying an intimate,
    night-time closeness.
  - Emotional Delivery: Nostalgic 'Itab (Gentle Reproach) and deep longing. It
    is a quiet lament addressed to the moon about a lover who forgot them. The
    delivery is not angry or harsh; it is purely heartbroken, full of yearning,
    and incredibly tender.

6. Lyrics with Tashkeel:

  - Dialect: Iraqi Folkloric / Poetic Baghdadi (incorporating warm Southern
    idioms like "Tarish" [Messenger] and utilizing "چ" [Ch] and "گ" [G]).

7. Technical Lyria Descriptor:

Early 1980s Iraqi studio orchestra era, slow swaying 4/4 Wahda rhythm at 75 BPM.
The instrumentation features a sweeping, cinematic string section (Kamanjat),
gentle acoustic Oud, sparkling Qanun glissandos, and a warm analog bassline. The
percussion is soft and atmospheric, utilizing a muffled Daf and gentle Darbuka
to create a boat-like swaying groove. The scale is a nostalgic Maqam Kurd,
evoking a sweet, night-time melancholy. A deep, velvety male baritone vocal
delivers deeply romantic Iraqi Arabic lyrics about the moon and unrequited love,
using slow, weeping vibrato and elongated, resonant phrasing. The emotional tone
is intimate, nostalgic, and gently sorrowful, processed with vintage analog
cassette warmth and a wide, lush studio reverb perfect for a late-night
listening experience.
