يا نجمة — حسين نعمة. Technical breakdown for the Lyria RAG system:

Historically, this 1969 track (composed by Kawkab Hamza with lyrics by Kadhim
Al-Rikabi) is considered a massive turning point in Iraqi music, marking the
birth of the "Modern Iraqi Song" (الأغنية الحديثة) by blending rural Southern
Iraqi sorrow with sophisticated, semi-Andalusian orchestral arrangements.

1. Metadata:

  - Artist/Title: Hussein Nima (حسين نعمة) - "Ya Najma" (يا نجمة)
  - Estimated Era: 1969 (The vanguard of the 1970s Iraqi Musical Renewal).

2. Maqam & Scale Analysis:

  - Primary Maqam: Maqam Bayati (Branching specifically into the Husseini color, which is a variation of Bayati that emphasizes the 5th degree and heavily characterizes Southern Iraqi laments).
  - Melodic Behavior: The Sair (melodic path) is incredibly spacious. The instrumental introduction is sweeping and cinematic, briefly ascending before the vocal enters. Hussein Nima’s melody frequently leaps up to address the "Star" (Najma) in the upper register, before cascading down in a slow, weeping resolution to the Bayati root note. The Tahrir (vocal ornamentation) here moves away from the harsh, guttural rural style of older generations into a smoother, elongated, sophisticated melisma.

3. Rhythm & Tempo (Iqa'):

  - Iqa' Name: A slow, deeply swung Wahda or highly stretched 4/4 Basta.
  - BPM: ~70-75 BPM.
  - Percussion Texture: Extremely sparse and atmospheric. A muted, resonant Daf (frame drum) handles a deeply delayed 'Dum' (downbeat), accompanied by delicate, syncopated Teks from a clay Darbuka and faint metallic shimmers from the Riq. The percussion purposefully leaves massive sonic space for the strings and the vocal to breathe.

4. Instrumentation:

  - Lead Instruments: Sweeping, layered Orchestral Violins (Kamanjat) drive the main melodic themes. The spaces between vocal phrases are filled by delicate Qanun glissandos and a soulful, breathy Nay (reed flute) that mimics the singer's sighs.
  - Ensemble Style: 1970s Baghdad Radio Orchestra (الفرقة الموسيقية للإذاعة). This track shifted Iraqi music away from the small traditional Takht (Oud, Joza, Santur) and into a lush, symphonic hybrid of Western string section arrangements playing microtonal Eastern scales.

5. Vocal Character:

  - Texture: A warm, silky, highly resonant Tenor/Baritone. Hussein Nima is historically dubbed the "Silken Voice of the South" (صوت الحرير) due to his smooth, enveloping timbre.
  - Emotional Delivery: Pure Southern Iraqi Huzn (melancholy). It is an intimate, night-time lament directed at a star. The delivery sounds deeply nostalgic, lonely, and exhausted by longing, feeling like an evolution of the traditional rural Abudhiya (poetry of pain) smoothed out for modern radio.

6. Lyrics with Tashkeel:

  - Dialect: Southern Iraqi Folkloric (Nasiriyah dialect), heavily featuring the "Ch" [چ] for Kaf and "G" [گ] for Qaf.
