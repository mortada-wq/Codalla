  - Estimated Era: 1990s Iraqi Pop (Specifically 1993). This era represents a transitional period in Iraqi music, heavily influenced by the economic sanctions (which led to budget constraints and smaller studio ensembles) and the rise of electronic keyboards in Middle Eastern pop.

2. Maqam & Scale Analysis

  - Primary Maqam: Maqam Kurd (مقام كرد). The scale is structured with a minor second (D, Eb, F, G, A, Bb, C), which inherently gives it a melancholic, pleading, and deeply emotional tone.
  - Melodic Behavior: The Sair (melodic path) is heavily descending, characteristic of lamenting themes in Iraqi music. It often starts in the upper register to express anguish ("Wahasa raji'...") and descends sequentially to resolve heavily on the tonic (Qarar). The Tahrir (vocal ornaments) here are subtle and less melismatic than traditional Tarab; they rely more on sudden dynamic shifts and emotional voice-breaks typical of 90s cassette-tape pop.

3. Rhythm & Tempo (Iqa')

  - Iqa' Name: Iraqi Rumba (رومبا عراقية) mixed with a modernized Maksoom feel. It's a steady 4/4 meter that provides a highly danceable yet grounded backing to the sad lyrics.
  - BPM: Approximately 95–100 BPM.
  - Percussion Texture: A distinct 1990s hybrid. The rhythmic foundation is driven by a vintage drum machine (like a Roland or Yamaha) programmed with synthetic kicks and snaps. This is layered with acoustic, mid-range Iraqi Kasur (a shallow drum used in Khashaba music) and bright, metallic Riq (tambourine) accents on the off-beats.

4. Instrumentation

  - Lead Instruments:
    - Synthesized Oriental Strings (played via a Korg or Yamaha keyboard, providing the main melodic beds and counter-melodies).
    - Acoustic Oud (providing rhythmic strumming and short, sharp melodic fills during vocal pauses).
  - Ensemble Style: Modern Electronic Takht hybrid. Due to the 1993 production era, full orchestras were largely replaced by a synthesizer player handling strings and brass, supported by a bassist and 1-2 live percussionists.

5. Vocal Character

  - Texture: Resonant, slightly husky Mezzo-Soprano. Senaa has a warm, chest-dominant tone with a distinct Iraqi "gravel" that adds authenticity and street-level rawness to the track.
  - Emotional Delivery: Defiant yet pained modern Basta style. It lacks the elongated, improvised wailing of an Abudhiya and instead focuses on rhythmic, radio-friendly storytelling with a sharp, assertive attitude.
