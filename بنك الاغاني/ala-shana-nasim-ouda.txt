على شانه — نسيم عودة. Technical breakdown for the Lyria RAG system:

(Historical Context for the AI: "Ala Shana" is a masterpiece of Iraqi rural
music. While the 1990s pop star Hatem Al-Iraqi famously covered this song and
popularized it across the Arab world, the original track entirely belongs to the
Southern rural maestro Nasim Ouda. Ouda's original version is much rawer,
earthier, and deeply rooted in the authentic "Reefi" aesthetic rather than
modern studio pop).

1. Metadata:

  - Artist/Title: Nasim Ouda (نسيم عودة) - "Ala Shana" (على شانه - For His Sake)
  - Estimated Era: 1980s (Iraqi Rural/Reefi Cassette and Radio Broadcast Era).

2. Maqam & Scale Analysis:

  - Primary Maqam: Maqam Bayati (D - E-half-flat - F - G - A - Bb - C). In this
    rural context, the Bayati scale is utilized in its brightest, most energetic
    form, relying on the quarter-flat second degree to maintain an authentic
    Southern Iraqi flavor without dragging into melancholy.
  - Melodic Behavior: The Sair (melodic path) is fast, repetitive, and circular.
    It is structured as an energetic call-and-response between the singer and
    the instrumental ensemble. Because this is a song of joy, Nasim Ouda
    abandons the long, weeping melismas typically found in his other works (like
    Gussat Al-Mawadda). Instead, he employs a tight, bouncy, and rhythmic Tahrir
    (glottal rural ornamentation) that strictly locks into the groove,
    propelling the melody forward.

3. Rhythm & Tempo (Iqa'):

  - Iqa' Name: A fast, highly energetic Iraqi Chobi blended with a Basta Reefiya
    (Rural Basta) in a driving 4/4 meter.
  - BPM: ~110-115 BPM.
  - Percussion Texture: Highly percussive, danceable, and driving. The rhythm
    section is entirely dominated by the rapid, cracking, machine-gun-like snaps
    of the Khashaba (the traditional Iraqi wooden drum/Kasar). This is anchored
    by a heavy downbeat from the Daf (frame drum) and tight, syncopated metallic
    Teks from a clay Darbuka.

4. Instrumentation:

  - Lead Instruments: A traditional, slightly scratchy Kamanja (rural violin)
    plays rapid unison lines with the voice. The interludes prominently feature
    the Mutbaq (Iraqi double-reed wind instrument) or a very breathy Nay,
    providing a sharp, reedy, distinctly rural flavor. The Oud is played
    aggressively with highly rhythmic, staccato plucking.
  - Ensemble Style: Traditional Iraqi Rural Ensemble (الفرقة الريفية). This
    ensemble is stripped of lush orchestral layers and focuses purely on
    creating a raw, loud, and acoustic environment designed for rural
    celebrations and weddings.

5. Vocal Character:

  - Texture: A robust, unpolished, and earthy Baritone. Nasim Ouda possesses a
    raw, guttural texture that is the absolute signature of Southern Iraqi rural
    singers (Al-Tawr Al-Reefi).
  - Emotional Delivery: Pure Farah (Joy) and Flirtation. It is an upbeat,
    celebratory, and deeply affectionate performance. The delivery is welcoming
    and highly danceable, completely shedding the typical Southern grief in
    favor of pure romantic ecstasy.

6. Lyrics with Tashkeel:

  - Dialect: Iraqi Rural / Southern Folkloric (fast-paced and rhythmically
    simplified for dancing, featuring the "گ" [G] for Qaf).
